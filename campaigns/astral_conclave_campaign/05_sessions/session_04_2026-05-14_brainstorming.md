## Relay Frequencies
- Each field relay operates on a distinct command frequency or resonance signature.
- The party's repaired detector is currently tuned to Sandglass because it followed the Sandglass leak here.
- Sandglass's surviving outbound channel contains the next frequency in the handoff chain: Cinderhook.
- Cinderhook's frequency is not just a name or map coordinate. It is a repeating harmonic cadence the detector can follow once retuned.
- If the party only recovers the frequency, they can pursue Cinderhook at normal detector range.

## Tuning Command Hardware
- The party can tune compatible command hardware to a known frequency once they recover that frequency from a relay core, leader wrist device, relay shard, or active signal.
- At Sandglass, this has to be a jury-rigged receiver tune. The site had proper retuning hardware during operation, but the evacuation crew removed it, so the party cannot cleanly retune Sandglass itself or use it as a full broadcast station.
- Tunable hardware:
  - repaired command-bolt detector,
  - loose command bolt fragments,
  - intact command bolts removed from victims,
  - relay shards or crystal vanes,
  - stupor-field device fragments,
  - sleeper-agent devices only if removed, inert, or safely isolated.
- Unsafe hardware:
  - active command bolts still attached to living victims,
  - sleeper devices still embedded in someone,
  - any device currently receiving live commands from an unknown source.
- Basic tuning procedure:
  - identify the target frequency,
  - ground the device with a brass timing ring, Reclaimer frame, antenna mount, or similar conductor,
  - align the crystal vane, command filament, or intact energy crystal to the frequency cadence,
  - test with a low-power ping before opening a stronger signal path.
- Suggested check:
  - DC 14 Tinker's Tools, Arcana, or Investigation to tune the repaired detector to a recovered frequency,
  - DC 15 Tinker's Tools or Arcana to tune a loose command bolt fragment,
  - DC 16 Medicine, Tinker's Tools, or Arcana to safely tune or read a removed command bolt,
  - DC 18 Medicine, Arcana, or Sovereign resonance to interact with a sleeper device without waking it.
- Time:
  - 1 minute for a rough tune if the frequency is clear and the device is already prepared,
  - 10 minutes for a stable field tune,
  - 1 hour for careful tuning that minimizes risk to nearby connected victims.
- Selix can grant advantage on tuning checks involving command bolts, damaged medical-control hardware, or victim safety.
- Mounted Antenna Benefit: if the Sandglass receiving antenna is installed on the Reclaimer, tuning checks to known relay frequencies have advantage and can detect weaker downstream signals.
- On Success:
  - the device locks to the chosen frequency,
  - the detector can follow that frequency,
  - a command bolt fragment can act as a key, tuner, or sacrificial component,
  - and the party can attempt a Feedback Spike on that frequency if they have a live signal path.
- On Strong Success:
  - the device can remember two nearby frequencies, though only one can be active at a time,
  - the party can switch between remembered frequencies with an action or short procedure,
  - or the tuning reveals whether living minds are attached to the active relay branch.
- On Failure:
  - the device locks to the wrong channel,
  - the detector points back toward Sandglass,
  - the hardware emits a painful command tone,
  - the device burns out one fragile component,
  - or the Conclave receives a weak ping that someone is probing the frequency.
- On Major Failure:
  - a command bolt or sleeper device briefly wakes,
  - a victim-side connection opens for one round,
  - the device emits an involuntary command pulse,
  - or the frequency becomes unstable and cannot be used for a safe Feedback Spike until repaired.
- Important Limit: tuning a command bolt does not automatically let the party issue commands. It gives them a receiver key, detector key, or spike key for that frequency. Sending believable commands requires separate access, authority, or deception.

## Retuning To Cinderhook At The Table
Use this once the party has recovered Cinderhook's frequency from Sandglass's outbound channel scar.

- Required pieces:
  - repaired command-bolt detector,
  - Cinderhook frequency pattern from the relay core, relay contact coil, crystal vane, intact energy crystal, or brass timing ring,

- Step 1: Isolate Sandglass.
  - The current detector still wants to listen to Sandglass's leaking pulse.
  - A character must dampen or mark the Sandglass cadence so the detector stops treating it as the primary signal.
  - Easy version: this happens automatically if they removed a good crystal vane or timing ring from the core.
  - Check version: DC 13 Arcana, Investigation, or Tinker's Tools.
- Step 2: Set The Cinderhook Cadence.
  - Align the detector's cracked inner ring with the Cinderhook frequency pattern.
  - The frequency presents as a repeating pulse, not a compass bearing: two short clicks, a rising hum, then a hollow pause.
  - The intact energy crystals can be made to vibrate at the Cinderhook cadence and act as a tuning fork for the receiver.
  - Problem: the portable detector was not designed to hold or read those crystals. The grounded Sandglass relay device was.
  - The party has to jury-rig a crystal clamp, wire cradle, or timing-ring socket to make the detector listen through the energy crystal.
  - Suggested check: DC 14 Tinker's Tools, Arcana, or Investigation. Selix can help.
- Step 3: Ground The Signal.
  - Clip or press the detector against a grounded conductor so the old Sandglass feedback does not overload the new tuning.
  - Best option: mount or wire it through the Sandglass receiving antenna on the Reclaimer.
  - Field option: hold it against the brass timing ring, basalt clamp, or Reclaimer hull.
  - Best jury-rig: bolt the energy crystal into the grounded antenna frame, then run a short lead into the detector.
- Step 4: Confirm The Lock.
  - On success, the detector stops twitching toward Sandglass and pulls in a new direction.
  - The pulse should feel cleaner but more distant.
  - If the antenna is mounted, the party also hears whether Cinderhook is steady, retuning, or starting to go dark.
- Fast Retune:
  - Takes 1 minute.
  - DC 14.
  - Good enough to follow Cinderhook, but vulnerable to sudden signal loss, crystal rattle, or decoy channels.
- Stable Retune:
  - Takes 10 minutes.
  - DC 14 with advantage if Selix helps or the antenna is mounted.
  - Lets the detector follow Cinderhook reliably and supports a later Feedback Spike on that frequency, but still counts as a field jury-rig.
- Careful Retune:
  - Takes 1 hour.
  - DC 14 with advantage if Selix helps.
  - Also lets the party check whether living minds are attached to Cinderhook's active branch before using the frequency aggressively.
- Failure:
  - The detector keeps snapping back to Sandglass,
  - or follows an old dead channel instead of Cinderhook,
  - or the jury-rigged energy crystal vibrates out of alignment,
  - or emits a painful command tone that alerts careful listeners.
- Strong Success:
  - The detector remembers both Sandglass and Cinderhook, though only one can be active at a time.
  - Switching between them later takes an action or a short field procedure.

## Why The Core Was Left Behind And Why The Scorpions Surfaced
- The relay core was not a portable computer or command archive. It was the signal-transfer anchor that synchronized Sandglass Relay with Heliox's larger command lattice.
- To make the desert broadcast stable, the Conclave bolted the core into basalt and used it as a heat sink and resonance ground.
- When the party destroyed the broadcasting device aboard [[The Calyxis Reclaimer]], it did not simply stop transmitting.
- Because the broadcaster and relay core were built from pre-war obedience technology, the Conclave operators did not realize the destroyed device would throw a feedback signal back along the active command stream.
- That feedback pulse hit Sandglass Relay before the crew fully understood what was happening.
- The surge overloaded the relay core, cracked its basalt grounding layer, and blew open the rock shelf beneath the site.
- The giant scorpions were already living below that sealed rock layer. They came through the new fissure into the heat, noise, and command residue around the core.
- The scorpions surfaced while the crew was trying to purge records and extract the damaged core.
- The Conclave crew cut their losses, fled through the emergency transit route, and left the core half-detached, partly melted, and fused into its mounting.
- This is why Sandglass still had a detectable signal after abandonment: the interrupted feedback event left the core damaged, half-active, and leaking relay residue.

## Feedback Weaponization Option
- Working name: Feedback Spike.
- Required components:
  - repaired command-bolt detector,
  - a Sandglass relay shard or timing ring,
  - access to an active command signal, relay core, command bolt, or stupor-field device,
  - and a device tuned to the specific frequency being targeted.
- Basic use: A character spends an action or a short field procedure to tune the detector to one known frequency, overload the linked component, and send feedback into that frequency's active command network. The feedback travels only as far as the next relay station or major command node on that frequency, then grounds out violently and blows that node.
- Suggested check: DC 16 Arcana, Tinker's Tools, or Sovereign resonance. Selix can grant advantage if present and helping.
- On Success, choose one effect appropriate to the target:
  - burn out a command bolt or stupor-field device,
  - stun or disorient controlled constructs for 1 round,
  - force an active relay to reveal its bearing or handoff target,
  - interrupt a warning pulse or purge sequence,
  - crack or blow the next relay core enough to make extraction or sabotage easier.
- On Strong Success, choose one additional benefit:
  - the feedback stays contained and does not damage the detector,
  - the party gains a clearer bearing toward the next relay,
  - one controlled victim gets a brief memory flash from before the command took hold,
  - or the Conclave does not immediately realize the spike came from the party.
- On Failure, choose one complication:
  - the detector burns out until repaired during a short rest,
  - nearby constructs take psychic or lightning damage,
  - controlled devices emit one final command pulse,
  - the Conclave receives a clear intrusion alert,
  - the target relay detects the spike forming and jumps to a backup frequency,
  - or the feedback rebounds into the Reclaimer and temporarily disables one vehicle subsystem.
- On a major failure, the feedback is uncontrolled and behaves like the Sandglass accident: useful damage at the next relay, but collateral cracking, heat, signal pain, uncontrolled command tones, or lethal backwash through attached victims.
- Grave Side Effect:
  - The Feedback Spike stops at the next relay station or major command node and blows it.
  - If warforged or constructs are still attached to that relay through command bolts, sleeper-agent devices, or active stupor-field links, the relay blowout may kill them.
  - The effect does not distinguish cleanly between Conclave hardware and victims being controlled by that hardware at the moment the relay fails.
  - A weak spike might only stun, burn out, or damage attached victims.
  - A strong, ungrounded, or deliberately weaponized spike might kill every attached victim in that relay's active signal branch.
  - This makes the Feedback Spike an emergency weapon, not a clean liberation tool.
- Possible Mitigations:
  - identify how many living minds are attached to the next relay before firing,
  - sever or ground victim-side connections first,
  - tune the spike to blow only the relay core rather than its attached victim channels,
  - use Selix's medical/mechanical expertise to reduce harm,
  - use a Sovereign interface to isolate hardware from living recipients,
  - or accept a lower-damage pulse that disables the target less completely but gives connected victims a chance to survive.
- Limits:
  - It only works on active or recently active command hardware.
  - It needs a connection or a strong signal bearing; it cannot target any Conclave device anywhere.
  - It can only affect the single frequency the detector is currently tuned to.
  - Retuning to another known frequency takes time and a check, and may be impossible if that frequency has not been recovered yet.
  - If the target relay changes frequency before the spike lands, the spike may miss, hit the old channel, or only damage abandoned hardware.
  - It always stops at the next relay or major command node; it cannot skip Cinderhook to hit Mirrordune unless Cinderhook is already offline or bypassed.
  - Repeated use risks destroying the repaired detector or the Sandglass shard.
  - It is dangerous around friendly warforged, sleeper devices, controlled victims, or the Reclaimer unless carefully grounded.

  - a supposed witness in protective dormancy,
  - seized relay hardware from Cinderhook or Mirrordune,
  - a way to clear Selix or Varyn,
  - or a route to prove the Reclaimer was controlled.
- Once the party enters, the Conclave tries to:
  - seal exits with civic lockdown doors,
  - flood the chamber with a localized stupor/control field,
  - activate compromised Heliox security constructs,
  - overload charged crystals or relay capacitors,
  - isolate the party from witnesses,
  - and purge evidence while the fight happens.
- The trap should use Heliox infrastructure, not obvious Conclave branding.
- The Conclave wants the official report to read: "Unauthorized intruders triggered unstable pre-war systems during emergency review."

#### Preparation 5 - Witness Quieting
- Workers tied to Mirrordune, Selix's factory memory, or the relay work orders are reassigned, sedated, or placed under sealed preservation review.
- Some are not killed because dead workers create records and questions.
- Instead, they are made unavailable, confused, or procedurally inaccessible.
- Public explanation: "protective dormancy," "stability review," or "emergency resource reassignment."

#### Preparation 6 - Anti-Feedback Measures
- The Conclave knows the Reclaimer feedback damaged Sandglass.
- They do not fully understand how the party might reproduce it, but they are afraid of it.
- In Heliox, they prepare:
  - frequency jumps,
  - sacrificial decoy channels,
  - grounded repeaters,
  - victim-side buffers,
  - and orders to sever local devices if a spike is detected.
- If the party tries a Feedback Spike inside Heliox, the Conclave may redirect it into an empty municipal repeater, but doing so may reveal the hidden command architecture.

#### Preparation 7 - The Citywide Last Resort
- If containment fails, the Conclave prepares to activate the citywide takeover field.
- They prefer not to use it because it consumes charged crystals, exposes their access, and risks damaging the [[Civic Directive Lattice]].
- They may begin with a partial district activation:
  - lock down the party's district,
  - turn patrols toward "public preservation,"
  - silence witnesses,
  - and move charged crystals or key personnel away.
- Full citywide activation happens only if the party is about to expose or break the compromised command structure.

#### Preparation 8 - Sovereign Contingency
- The Conclave has noticed enough to know the construct PC is anomalous.
- They do not know exactly why the Sovereign resists the control field or what authority the Sovereign can exert over old systems.
- Their plan is to kill the party before they can understand the Sovereign's importance.
- Possible tactics:
  - lure the Sovereign toward an old Lattice interface,
  - flood the interface with memory noise,
  - frame the Sovereign as dangerous pre-war authority,
  - or use the Sovereign's immunity as proof that the party is causing the civic disturbance.
- The Conclave would prefer to learn from the Sovereign, but if the kill trap works, they will accept destroying the anomaly.

### What The Conclave Does Not Expect
- The Sovereign can exert override authority over some old Heliox or pre-war systems.
- The Conclave assumes the Sovereign is merely resistant to obedience signals, not able to command infrastructure back.
- In the kill box, this can become the party's escape hatch or reversal.
- Possible Sovereign override effects:
  - unlock one sealed civic door,
  - force a Lattice repeater to ignore Conclave command inserts,
  - ground a localized stupor field for 1 minute,
  - turn a security construct from hostile to neutral,
  - redirect a crystal overload into a safe grounding channel,
  - reveal the true command path behind the false Lattice access panel,
  - or broadcast a short citywide contradiction that makes nearby citizens hesitate instead of obey.
- Suggested trigger:
  - the Sovereign touches an old access column, civic relay spine, recharge regulator, or emergency override console while under threat.
- Suggested DC:
  - DC 15 for a small local override,
  - DC 18 for a room-scale reversal,
  - DC 20+ for anything that affects multiple city systems or exposes the Conclave.
- Cost or complication:
  - memory flash,
  - psychic pain,
  - the Lattice marks the Sovereign as an active authority,
  - the Conclave learns the Sovereign can override more than they expected,
  - or the override protects the party but accelerates citywide takeover escalation.

### Conclave Escalation Ladder In Heliox
- Stage 1: Watch the party through civic checkpoints and wrist-device reports.
- Stage 2: Delay and redirect them with legitimate procedure.
- Stage 3: Separate witnesses, hardware, and the Reclaimer.
- Stage 4: Lure the party into an approved investigation route.
- Stage 5: Spring the kill box and destroy or seize their evidence.
- Stage 6: If they survive, discredit them as Calyxis saboteurs or pre-war contamination vectors.
- Stage 7: Trigger district-level control through the Lattice.
- Stage 8: Activate the citywide takeover field as a last resort.

### Citywide Takeover Field
- The [[Astral Conclave]] can, if desperate, use the [[Civic Directive Lattice]] to broadcast a citywide control field across [[Heliox Prime]].
- This is not active when the party first enters the city.
- Heliox does not know the city can be taken over this way.
- The Conclave treats it as a last resort because it requires a massive burst of stored energy, likely from charged crystals routed through civic reserve systems.
- It also risks damaging the Lattice, exposing the Conclave's access, and burning through resources they need for larger plans.
- If activated, the field does not need to issue detailed commands to every person. It makes affected warforged/construct citizens receptive, slows independent resistance, and lets addressed wrist-device orders or Lattice commands take priority.
- The horror should be gradual at first:
  - public dispatch bells ring out of sequence,
  - workers pause mid-task,
  - patrols stop speaking and turn together,
  - maintenance doors unlock or seal in waves,
  - and citizens begin obeying directives they do not remember hearing.

### Takeover Field Triggers
Use one only if the party pushes hard enough or the Conclave believes exposure is imminent.

- The party exposes the Heliox-side command source in public.
- The party moves to seize or destroy the Lattice access point.
- The party brings proof to an authority who is not compromised.
- The party interrupts the charged-crystal routing from Mirrordune.
- The Sovereign interfaces with the Lattice and the Conclave panics.
- A captured technician or compromised supervisor reveals too much.

### How The Players Can Learn It Exists
- Energy routing records show impossible reserve draw capacity labeled as emergency preservation load.
- A maintenance schematic shows the Lattice can emit through every dispatch tower at once, even though normal dispatches do not require that.
- Mirrordune charge ledgers show too many charged crystals being moved into civic systems for ordinary rationing.
- A sympathetic Heliox technician notices "signal smoothing" upgrades are wired to broadcast, not just receive.
- Selix recognizes a stupor-field geometry scaled up across civic relay towers.
- Varyn remembers a repeated phrase or dispatch cadence that was not meant for ordinary workers.
- The Sovereign can feel an old authority layer in the city refusing the Conclave's newer obedience logic.

### Who In Heliox Might Know Something Is Wrong
- Most of Heliox does not know and would reject the claim at first.
- A few people may suspect enough to help:
  - a maintenance archivist who saw unauthorized Lattice modifications,
  - a dispatch clerk whose route tables changed without authorization,
  - a refinement worker who lost time near the crystal logistics annex,
  - a patrol officer whose squad repeated an order nobody issued,
  - or a civic engineer loyal to Heliox who sees the party as outsiders with less to lose.
- These allies do not know the whole plot. They know the city has been made vulnerable.

### Civic Unease
- Heliox citizens are disciplined, but they are not blind.
- Many still trust the Preservation Doctrine, but they are starting to question their leaders' recent activity.
- Common worries:
  - too many workers reassigned under sealed authority,
  - crystal logistics moving at odd hours,
  - patrols rerouted away from certain maintenance districts,
  - dispatches using unfamiliar cadence or phrasing,
  - ration justifications changing without public calculation,
  - and leaders refusing to explain emergency preservation measures.
- Most citizens frame the problem as secrecy, overreach, or leadership panic, not Astral Conclave control.
- This creates an opening for the party:
  - skeptical citizens may share rumors,
  - civic workers may point to suspicious access records,
  - and loyal Heliox personnel may help if the party proves the city itself is being used.
- It also creates danger:
  - public unrest gives the Conclave an excuse to tighten control,
  - panicked leaders may deny everything,
  - and the citywide takeover field becomes more tempting as a last-resort "restore order" measure.

### Surviving A Citywide Activation
- If the field activates, the party's goal becomes movement, concealment, and targeted sabotage rather than fighting the whole city.
- Options:
  - hide inside unpowered maintenance shafts,
  - move through old Sovereign-era service tunnels not fully tied to the Lattice,
  - destroy or disable local dispatch repeaters to create safe pockets,
  - use tuned crystals to identify which towers are carrying the strongest control signal,
  - sever charged-crystal feeds before the field stabilizes,
  - free a small group of controlled citizens by grounding their local relay,
  - or reach the Lattice access point and break the Conclave insert.
- Destroying repeaters can protect the party and nearby citizens but may damage Heliox infrastructure and make the party look like saboteurs.
- Hiding can keep the party alive but gives the Conclave time to move crystals, purge evidence, or target allies.

### Why The Sovereign Is Not Affected
- The construct PC's Sovereign nature does not match normal Heliox civic obedience profiles.
- Possible explanation: the field is built to exploit post-war Heliox command architecture, while the Sovereign predates or outranks that structure.
- The Lattice recognizes the Sovereign as an authority profile, not a receiver profile.
- The Conclave can detect the anomaly, but it cannot simply overwrite it with the citywide field.
- The Sovereign may still suffer pressure:
  - pain from the command cadence,
  - memory flashes,
  - attention from the Lattice,
  - or the risk of becoming a visible beacon to the Conclave.
- Useful reveal line: `ACCORD EXECUTIVE PROFILE DETECTED. CIVIC OBEDIENCE CHANNEL INAPPLICABLE.`

### Takeover Field Costs And Limits
- It consumes a massive amount of charged-crystal energy.
- It cannot be held indefinitely without damaging the Lattice or draining reserves.
- It works best through dispatch towers, civic repeaters, and connected public systems.
- It is weaker in old tunnels, shielded industrial vaults, damaged districts, and places disconnected from civic power.
- It is less precise than individual command bolts or wrist-device orders.
- It may not affect all constructs equally:
  - heavily damaged citizens may receive only static,
  - older pre-war constructs may resist,
  - non-construct party members may feel pressure but are not directly controlled,
  - and the Sovereign is not controlled by it.

### Heliox Choices
- Investigate the [[Civic Directive Lattice]] access point.
- Follow Selix's memory toward an energy-core refinement district.
- Track where Mirrordune's charged crystals are being routed.
- Warn Heliox authorities and risk tipping off compromised officials.
- Return to Calyxis with proof that Heliox infrastructure is compromised.
- Leave Heliox and chase Mirrordune before the charging-site evidence is gone.

### What Not To Reveal Yet
- Do not reveal the full identity of the Heliox access-provider unless you want to accelerate the arc.
- Do not make all Heliox citizens collaborators.
- Do not fully solve the [[Civic Directive Lattice]] in one scene.
- Do not reveal the final destination of all charged crystals unless the party commits hard to the Heliox branch.
- Do not activate the citywide takeover field immediately on arrival. Let the party discover the possibility first, then trigger it only if the Conclave chooses last-resort escalation.

## Sovereign Network Override Option
Use this if the construct PC interfaces with the detector, the relay hardware, or the damaged pre-war relay contact coil.

- Suggested DC: 15 at Sandglass Relay because the site is mostly purged but still resonant.
- On Success, choose one:
  - trace the relay-to-relay route toward Cinderhook,
  - identify which signal came from Sandglass Relay and which signal was handed onward,
  - send a false "stable and compliant" pulse to buy time,
  - shape the feedback mechanism into a controlled Feedback Spike,
  - confirm that an examined sleeper device is inert and cannot receive new commands,
  - recover one pre-control memory fragment from Selix, Varyn, or the compromised Calyxis soldier.
- On Strong Success, choose one:
  - identify Cinderhook clearly and catch a weaker downstream signal from Mirrordune,
  - lock out the abandoned relay signature so it cannot mislead the detector again,
  - ground the Feedback Spike so it can be used once without risking the Reclaimer,
  - identify the command architecture as late-war forbidden obedience technology.
- On Failure, choose one:
  - the Conclave receives a ping that a Sovereign-level resonance touched the network,
  - the detector emits a painful command tone from the abandoned relay path,
  - the detector briefly points back toward the party instead of the desert,
  - the construct PC receives a painful memory spike about warforged obedience design.
